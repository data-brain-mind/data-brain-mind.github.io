Data on the Brain & Mind

Concrete Applications of AI to Neuroscience and Cognitive Science
NeurIPS 2025, San Diego CA

Overview

Our workshop aims to connect machine learning researchers with neuroscientists and cognitive scientists by focusing on concrete, open problems grounded in emerging datasets of neural and/or behavioral data in humans and other animals. As in other computational sciences, these large-scale, open datasets are transforming the practice of research in a variety of subdomains, including whole-brain circuitry, visual-motor cortical processing, human development, and natural speech processing. We believe that a workshop focused on concrete and accessible problems via open datasets can catalyze timely and high-impact connections among domain scientists in neuroscience and cognitive science and ML practitioners. Our data-driven approach is motivated by substantial existing work on ML for neuroscience and cognitive science, including in data analysis and in the development of normative models. Foundational work has already established conceptual frameworks and standardized evaluations that have advanced our understanding of neural computation for cognition. Combined with the growth of high-resolution, large-scale datasets in both neuroscience and cognitive science, we believe a workshop focused on concrete, applied problems is timely and presents a clear opportunity to forge collaborations. We envision outcomes such as applying ML to large behavioral datasets, analyzing clinical conditions like Parkinson's disease through reinforcement learning frameworks, and developing ML models for stroke and grasping studies.

Contributed Work

We invite 4-page submissions in three categories:

  • Dataset Papers: Submissions should present neuroscience or cognitive science datasets that clearly articulate open scientific questions the dataset could address, highlight connections to relevant computational or machine learning approaches, provide well-formatted and accessible data (such as pandas DataFrames or standardized formats), and may include previously published datasets if they identify new questions. Examples of dataset papers include: neural recording datasets with specific hypotheses about computation, behavioral datasets with questions about learning mechanisms, and clinical datasets with therapeutic targets.
  • Method Papers: Submissions should present machine learning methods with clear applications to neuroscience or cognitive science, including novel algorithms with potential for experimental testing, adaptations of existing methods to domain-specific problems, theoretical frameworks that could guide experimental design, and tools for standardized evaluation—building toward an "ImageNet for neuroscience."
  • Problem Statements: We welcome position papers and proposals of untested ideas that identify concrete open problems requiring interdisciplinary collaboration, propose experimental frameworks informed by computational theory, suggest new evaluation paradigms or benchmarks, and aim to bridge gaps between the neuroscience, cognitive science, and machine learning communities.
